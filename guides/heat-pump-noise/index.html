Heat Pump Noise: How Loud Are They Really?

A typical air source heat pump produces 40 to 50 dB at one metre distance. That is roughly the volume of a quiet conversation or a modern fridge. At your neighbour's window (typically 5 to 10 metres away), it drops to 30 to 40 dB, which is quieter than background noise in most residential areas. Here is the full picture.

How loud is a heat pump in real terms?

Heat pump noise is measured in decibels (dB). Most air source heat pumps produce 40 to 50 dB at one metre from the outdoor unit. This is the sound level you would hear standing right next to it. As you move away, the noise drops significantly because sound dissipates with distance.

At 5 metres (a typical distance to a garden boundary), the level drops to approximately 32 to 42 dB. At 10 metres (a typical distance to a neighbour's window in a semi-detached street), it drops to 26 to 36 dB. For context, a quiet bedroom at night is around 30 dB and normal conversation at one metre is 60 dB.

The character of the sound matters too. Heat pumps produce a low, steady hum from the compressor and a gentle whooshing from the fan. It is not a sharp, intermittent, or high-pitched noise. Most people find it far less intrusive than traffic noise, which is typically 50 to 70 dB in residential areas.

One important detail: your existing gas boiler is often louder than a heat pump, but you do not notice it because it is inside your house. The gas boiler in a kitchen or utility room typically produces 45 to 60 dB. A heat pump at 5 metres outside is quieter than the boiler you already live with.

Planning permission and noise limits

Most domestic air source heat pumps qualify as permitted development under the Microgeneration Certification Scheme (MCS) standards. This means you do not need planning permission provided the noise level does not exceed 42 dB at one metre from the nearest neighbour's habitable room window.

Your MCS-certified installer is required to perform a noise assessment as part of the installation design. This assessment calculates the expected noise level at the nearest neighbour's property based on the unit's sound power rating, the distance, and any barriers. If the 42 dB limit cannot be met with standard positioning, the installer may recommend acoustic barriers, anti-vibration mounts, or a different unit position.

Additional planning restrictions apply to listed buildings, properties in conservation areas, World Heritage Sites, and Areas of Outstanding Natural Beauty. In these cases, you may need to submit a planning application. Contact your local planning authority to check.

Can neighbours complain about heat pump noise?

Technically, yes. Any noise source can be reported as a statutory nuisance under the Environmental Protection Act 1990. However, a heat pump that meets MCS installation standards is extremely unlikely to breach noise limits. The 42 dB permitted development limit is deliberately set below the level that would constitute a nuisance.

In practice, most neighbour concerns come from incorrect expectations or poor installation. Common issues include: the unit placed too close to a boundary without adequate clearance, hard surfaces nearby that reflect and amplify sound, the unit mounted directly on a wall without anti-vibration pads (transmitting vibrations into the building structure), or an older, noisier model installed when a quieter one was available.

If you are worried about neighbour relations, talk to them before installation. Explain what the unit will sound like (show them a decibel comparison), where it will be positioned, and that it meets strict noise limits. Most objections come from uncertainty, not actual noise experience.

Are heat pumps noisy at night?

Heat pumps are quieter at night than during the day. Modern inverter-driven units modulate their output based on demand. On a cold winter night, the heat pump runs at lower capacity because you need less heat while sleeping (especially if you set back the thermostat slightly). At 50% capacity, noise drops by approximately 3 to 6 dB compared to full output.

A typical unit running overnight produces 35 to 42 dB at one metre, dropping to 25 to 32 dB at the garden boundary. For comparison, the World Health Organization recommends a maximum of 45 dB outside a bedroom window for undisturbed sleep. A properly positioned heat pump is well below this threshold.

If nighttime noise is a particular concern (for example, if your outdoor unit is near a bedroom window), consider scheduling the heat pump to pre-heat your home during the early evening and reduce output overnight. Smart heating controls and heat pump tariffs make this easy to manage.

How to reduce heat pump noise

Positioning. The single most effective measure. Place the unit as far from your own and neighbours' bedrooms as possible. Avoid corners where two walls meet, as sound can reflect and amplify. South or west-facing positions often work well because they are warmer (improving efficiency) and typically face away from neighbouring houses.

Anti-vibration mounts. Rubber or spring-loaded mounts prevent vibrations transmitting through the ground or wall. These should be standard on any quality installation. If your installer does not include them, ask.

Acoustic barriers. A solid fence, wall, or purpose-built acoustic enclosure between the unit and the boundary can reduce noise by 5 to 10 dB. The barrier needs to be taller than the unit and must not restrict airflow to the intake or exhaust.

Soft landscaping. Dense hedging or planting near the unit can absorb some sound, though the effect is modest (1 to 3 dB). It also improves the visual appearance.

Choose a quiet model. There is meaningful variation between models. Some premium units are rated at 38 dB while budget models may reach 52 dB. The difference is very noticeable. See the section below.

Quietest heat pump models

When comparing heat pump noise levels, look for the sound power level (LWA) in the product datasheet. Lower is better. Here are typical noise ranges by manufacturer for residential air source heat pumps.

Daikin Altherma 3 models are among the quietest at 38 to 46 dB depending on capacity. Vaillant aroTHERM plus ranges from 40 to 48 dB. Samsung EHS Mono is 40 to 46 dB. Grant Aerona3 is 42 to 48 dB. Mitsubishi Ecodan models range from 42 to 50 dB.

Your MCS-certified installer can recommend the quietest suitable model for your property size and heat demand. Do not choose a model based on noise alone, as efficiency and reliability matter more for long-term satisfaction. But if you have a choice between two equally suitable models, pick the quieter one.

Ground source heat pumps: the silent alternative

If noise is a major concern, a ground source heat pump eliminates it almost entirely. The compressor unit sits inside your house (typically in a utility room or garage) and the ground loops are buried underground. There is no external fan and no outdoor noise. Internal noise is similar to a gas boiler or washing machine.

Ground source heat pumps cost more (£15,000 to £35,000 before the £6,000 BUS grant) and require garden space for boreholes or trenches. But for properties in noise-sensitive locations, flats with close neighbours, or anyone who simply wants zero outdoor noise, they are worth considering. They also achieve higher efficiency (COP 4.0 to 5.0) than air source models. Use our heat pump calculator to compare costs.

Frequently asked questions

How loud is a heat pump?

40 to 50 dB at one metre, similar to a quiet conversation or modern fridge. At a neighbour's window (10 metres away), it drops to 26 to 36 dB, which is quieter than a library. Calculate your full heat pump costs.

Can neighbours complain about heat pump noise?

A properly installed heat pump meeting MCS standards (42 dB or less at the nearest neighbour's window) is very unlikely to breach noise limits. Talk to neighbours before installation to address concerns early.

Do heat pumps need planning permission for noise?

Most domestic heat pumps are permitted development and do not need planning permission, provided they meet the 42 dB noise limit at the nearest habitable room. Listed buildings and conservation areas may require a planning application.

Are heat pumps noisy at night?

They are quieter at night because they run at lower capacity. A typical unit at 50% output produces 35 to 42 dB at one metre, dropping to 25 to 32 dB at the boundary. This is well below WHO sleep guidelines of 45 dB.

Which heat pumps are the quietest?

Daikin, Vaillant, and Samsung models tend to be quietest at 38 to 46 dB. Ask your MCS-certified installer to recommend quiet models. Ground source heat pumps have zero outdoor noise.
