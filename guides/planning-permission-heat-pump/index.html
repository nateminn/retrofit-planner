Do I Need Planning Permission for a Heat Pump?

In most cases, no. Domestic air source heat pumps are classified as permitted development, meaning you do not need planning permission. But there are conditions you must meet, and certain properties (listed buildings, conservation areas, flats) have additional rules. Here is a complete guide.

Permitted development conditions for air source heat pumps

Under the Town and Country Planning (General Permitted Development) Order, you can install an air source heat pump without planning permission if all of the following conditions are met.

Noise limit. The heat pump must not produce noise exceeding 42 dB(A) at one metre from the nearest neighbouring habitable room window. Your MCS-certified installer performs a noise assessment as part of the installation design. See our heat pump noise guide for decibel comparisons and how to reduce noise. See recommended sound level meters to measure your specific situation.

One unit per property. Only one air source heat pump is permitted under these rules. If you need a second unit, you will need a planning application.

Not on a wall or roof fronting a highway. The unit cannot be installed on a wall or roof that faces and is visible from a road or public footpath. Side walls and rear walls are fine. This rule is about visual impact.

Standalone units. If freestanding (on the ground), the unit must comply with size limits: no more than 0.6 cubic metres for the outdoor unit.

Removal condition. When the heat pump is no longer needed, it must be removed and the property restored to its previous condition.

Listed buildings

Listed buildings require listed building consent for any external alteration, including a heat pump. This does not mean you cannot have one, but it means your local planning authority must approve the installation. The application should show where the unit will be placed, that it is not visible from the principal elevation, and that it does not damage historic fabric. Approval typically takes 8 to 12 weeks.

A ground source heat pump may be easier to approve for listed buildings because there is no external above-ground unit. The indoor equipment sits in a utility room and the ground loops are buried. See our ground source vs air source comparison.

Conservation areas

In conservation areas, permitted development rights for heat pumps still apply in most cases, but with an additional restriction: the unit must not be installed on a wall or roof that fronts a highway. If your property faces a road, you may need to place the unit in the rear garden or on a side wall that is not visible from the street. If this is not possible, you will need a planning application. See our full conservation area guide.

Ground source heat pumps

Ground source heat pumps are generally permitted development because there is no above-ground external equipment. The compressor unit sits inside your home (similar to a gas boiler in noise and size). Borehole drilling and trench excavation are covered under permitted development for domestic properties. The main exception is listed buildings, where any groundworks may require consent. Compare ground source costs.

Scotland and Wales

Planning rules differ slightly. In Scotland, permitted development rights for heat pumps are broadly similar but administered by your local council under Scottish planning legislation. In Wales, the rules mirror England but with Welsh-language planning guidance. In both cases, the 42 dB noise limit and single-unit rule apply. Contact your local planning authority for confirmation.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need planning permission for a heat pump?

Usually no. Most domestic heat pumps are permitted development if they meet the 42 dB noise limit, are the only unit on the property, and are not on a highway-facing wall. Calculate your costs.

Do I need planning permission in a conservation area?

Only if the unit would be on a wall or roof fronting a highway. Rear and side installations are usually fine. Contact your local planning office if unsure. Read our conservation area guide.

Do ground source heat pumps need planning permission?

Almost never, because there is no external above-ground unit. Listed buildings may need consent for groundworks. Compare ground vs air source.

What is the 42 dB noise rule?

The heat pump must not exceed 42 dB(A) at one metre from the nearest neighbour's habitable room window. Your MCS installer performs this assessment. Full noise guide.
