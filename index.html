How much does a heat pump cost in 2026?

The total cost of installing an air source heat pump in a typical UK home ranges from £7,000 to £13,000 before the government grant. After the £7,500 Boiler Upgrade Scheme grant, most homeowners pay between £500 and £5,500 out of pocket. Ground source heat pumps cost more — typically £15,000 to £35,000 before grants — but deliver higher efficiency.

The exact cost depends on your property size, how well insulated your home is, whether your radiators need upgrading, and the complexity of the installation. Our calculator uses data from the Energy Saving Trust and current Ofgem energy prices to give you a realistic estimate.

Heat pump running costs vs gas boiler

Heat pumps are more efficient than gas boilers because they transfer heat rather than generating it by burning fuel. A typical air source heat pump achieves a coefficient of performance (COP) of 2.8 to 3.5, meaning it produces 2.8 to 3.5 units of heat for every unit of electricity consumed.

However, electricity costs more per unit than gas. At current Ofgem rates (24.5p/kWh electricity vs 6.76p/kWh gas as of Q1 2026), a heat pump with a COP of 3.0 costs roughly 8.2p per kWh of heat — compared to around 7.5p per kWh from a gas boiler operating at 90% efficiency. The savings are modest for gas users but substantial for anyone on oil, LPG, or electric heating. For a detailed side-by-side analysis, see our boiler vs heat pump comparison tool.

Is a heat pump worth it?

For homes currently heated by oil or LPG, a heat pump almost always saves money from year one. For homes on mains gas, the financial case is tighter — the savings come primarily from the government grant reducing the upfront cost, and from the expectation that gas prices will rise relative to electricity as the UK decarbonises. The environmental case is clear: a heat pump in a well-insulated home typically reduces CO2 emissions by 50% to 70% compared to a gas boiler.

The best candidates for heat pumps are homes with good insulation, adequate space for the outdoor unit, and a willingness to accept slightly lower flow temperatures (which means larger radiators may be needed in some rooms). If your home's EPC rating is D or below, you should consider insulating first — this reduces the heat pump size needed and improves efficiency.

The Boiler Upgrade Scheme explained

The Boiler Upgrade Scheme (BUS) provides grants of £7,500 for air source heat pumps and £6,000 for ground source heat pumps in England and Wales. The scheme runs until March 2028. Your installer applies for the grant on your behalf, and it's deducted from your invoice — you never have to pay the full amount and claim back.

To qualify, your property must have a valid EPC (less than 10 years old), and the installation must be carried out by an MCS-certified installer. There is no income test or means test — the grant is available to all eligible homeowners. Frequently asked questions

How long does a heat pump last?

Air source heat pumps typically last 20 to 25 years with proper servicing — roughly double the lifespan of a gas boiler. This longer lifespan is factored into the lifetime cost comparison with a new gas boiler.

Do I need planning permission for a heat pump?

Most air source heat pump installations fall under permitted development rights and don't require planning permission, provided the unit meets noise limits and is positioned appropriately. Listed buildings and conservation areas may have additional restrictions.

Should I insulate my home before getting a heat pump?

Yes, in most cases. Better insulation reduces your heat demand, which means you can install a smaller and cheaper heat pump. It also improves the heat pump's efficiency and comfort levels. Our EPC improvement planner can show you the most cost-effective order of improvements.

Can I combine a heat pump with solar panels?

Yes, and it's a highly effective combination. Solar panels can generate electricity to power your heat pump during daylight hours, further reducing running costs. Some homeowners pair solar panels with a battery to maximise self-consumption.
