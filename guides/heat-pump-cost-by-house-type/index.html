Heat Pump Cost by House Type UK 2026

The cost of a heat pump depends heavily on your house type. A 2 bed flat might pay nothing after the grant. A 5 bed detached could pay £4,500 to £7,500. Here is a full breakdown for every common UK property type.

Heat pump for a 3 bed semi-detached house

The 3 bed semi is the most common UK house type, so this is the most-asked question. A typical 3 bed semi needs an 8 to 10 kW air source heat pump. Installation costs £9,000 to £12,000. After the £7,500 BUS grant, you pay £1,500 to £4,500.

Annual running costs are £650 to £950 in a well-insulated home, compared to £600 to £800 for a gas boiler. The difference is small. The financial case comes from the grant reducing the upfront cost to near-parity with a new gas boiler, plus the long-term expectation that gas prices will rise.

If your 3 bed semi is poorly insulated, insulate first. Cavity wall and loft insulation together cost £700 to £2,000 (often free through ECO4) and will reduce both your heat demand and the size of heat pump you need.

Heat pump for a 4 bed detached house

Detached houses have the highest heat demand because all four walls, the roof, and the floor are exposed to the outside. A 4 bed detached typically needs a 10 to 14 kW system costing £11,000 to £14,000. After the grant, you pay £3,500 to £6,500.

Running costs are £900 to £1,300 per year. This is where insulation makes the biggest difference. A well-insulated detached house can reduce its heat demand by 30% to 40% compared to a poorly insulated one, which means a smaller heat pump (cheaper to buy) running more efficiently (cheaper to operate). Heat pump for a flat

Flats are the cheapest to install because they are small and have fewer exposed surfaces. A 1 to 2 bed flat typically needs a 4 to 6 kW unit costing £7,000 to £9,000. After the £7,500 grant, many flat owners pay nothing or very little.

The catch is practical, not financial. You need somewhere to mount the outdoor unit, which requires permission from the freeholder or management company. The unit produces some noise (40 to 50 dB, similar to a fridge) which neighbours may object to. Some apartment blocks are installing communal heat pump systems instead, which avoid these issues.

Heat pump for a bungalow

Bungalows have a larger roof area relative to their floor space, which means more heat loss through the roof. However, they often have good loft access for insulation, and the single-storey layout makes heat pump installation straightforward. Costs are similar to a 3 bed semi: £9,000 to £12,000, or £1,500 to £4,500 after the grant.

Bungalows are particularly good candidates for underfloor heating, which works extremely well with heat pumps at low flow temperatures. If you are renovating a bungalow, consider underfloor heating as part of the project.

Heat pump for a terraced house

Mid-terrace houses are the cheapest to heat because they only have two exposed walls (front and back). This means lower heat demand and a smaller heat pump. A 3 bed mid-terrace needs an 8 to 10 kW system costing £9,000 to £11,000, or £1,500 to £3,500 after the grant.

End-terrace houses have one additional exposed wall, so heat demand is 10% to 15% higher. Costs are slightly higher at £9,000 to £11,500, or £1,500 to £4,000 after the grant.

For terraced houses, the main installation consideration is space for the outdoor unit. You need at least 1 metre clearance around the unit for airflow. Most terraced houses can accommodate this in a side return or back garden. Check planning permission requirements if you are in a conservation area.

What affects the cost beyond house type?

Insulation level. The biggest variable. A poorly insulated home needs a larger heat pump (more expensive) that works harder (higher running costs). Check your insulation savings before committing to a heat pump.

Existing heating system. Replacing a combi boiler means you also need a hot water cylinder (add £500 to £1,500). Replacing a system boiler is simpler because you already have a cylinder. Replacing electric heating is the most straightforward.

Radiator upgrades. Heat pumps work at lower temperatures than gas boilers. If your radiators are undersized, some may need upgrading. Budget £100 to £200 per radiator that needs replacing. Not all radiators will need upgrading.

Electrical supply. Most heat pumps run on a standard single-phase electrical supply. Larger systems (above 12 kW) may require a three-phase upgrade costing £1,000 to £3,000. Your installer will check this during the survey.

Frequently asked questions

How much is a heat pump for a 3 bed semi?

£9,000 to £12,000 installed, or £1,500 to £4,500 after the £7,500 BUS grant. Annual running costs are £650 to £950 in a well-insulated home. Calculate your exact cost.

How much is a heat pump for a 4 bed detached house?

£11,000 to £14,000 installed, or £3,500 to £6,500 after the grant. Running costs are £900 to £1,300 per year. Improving insulation first can reduce both the system size and running costs significantly.

Can you put a heat pump in a flat?

Yes. A flat typically needs a small 4 to 6 kW unit costing £7,000 to £9,000 (£0 to £1,500 after grant). You need freeholder permission for the outdoor unit and should consider noise impact on neighbours.

What size heat pump do I need for a 3 bed house?

Typically 8 to 10 kW for a well-insulated 3 bed house. 10 to 12 kW if poorly insulated. Your MCS-certified installer will perform a heat loss calculation to determine the exact size.
