Is Loft Insulation Worth It in 2026?

Yes. Loft insulation costs £300 to £600 and saves £135 to £355 per year. That is a payback period of 1 to 4 years. It lasts over 40 years with no maintenance. Many households get it free through ECO4. It is the single best value home improvement you can make.

The payback period of 1 to 4 years makes loft insulation the fastest-returning home improvement available. By comparison, solar panels pay back in 8 to 12 years and a heat pump in 5 to 15 years. Loft insulation should always be your first investment.

DIY vs professional installation

DIY. If your loft is accessible, unboarded, and you are comfortable working at height, laying mineral wool rolls is a straightforward weekend project. Materials cost £5 to £8 per square metre from builders' merchants (approximately £150 to £300 for a typical 3 bed house). You need a dust mask, gloves, and a board to kneel on across the joists. The recommended depth is 270mm, which means a 100mm layer between the joists and a 170mm layer on top, laid at right angles.

Professional. A certified installer charges £300 to £600 for a typical home. The work takes 1 to 2 hours. Professional installation is worth it if your loft is hard to access, has obstructions, or if you are not comfortable with the work. The installer also checks for ventilation issues and any existing problems.

Free through ECO4. If you receive means-tested benefits and your EPC rating is D or below, you may get professional installation at no cost through the ECO4 scheme. This is the best option if you qualify. Check your eligibility.

When loft insulation is not suitable

Loft insulation is not suitable if you have a converted loft (you need room-in-roof insulation instead), your loft has no access hatch or the hatch is too small (under 400mm), there are active damp or condensation problems that need resolving first, or the loft is fully boarded and you cannot lift the boards.

If your loft is converted, room-in-roof insulation costs more (£1,500 to £3,500) but is also available through ECO4 for qualifying households.

How thick should loft insulation be?

The recommended depth is 270mm of mineral wool. Current building regulations for new builds require 270mm minimum. If your loft already has some insulation but less than 100mm, topping up is still very cost-effective (saving 50% to 70% of what a full installation saves).

Going beyond 270mm gives diminishing returns. The difference between 270mm and 400mm is small in terms of annual savings, so the extra cost is rarely justified.

Effect on EPC rating

Loft insulation adds 5 to 10 points to your EPC rating. For landlords needing to meet minimum EPC standards, this is one of the cheapest routes to compliance. Combined with cavity wall insulation (10 to 15 points), you can jump one or even two EPC bands for under £2,000. See our guide to improving your EPC cheaply.

Insulate before installing a heat pump

If you are considering a heat pump, insulate your loft first. Better insulation means you need a smaller heat pump (lower cost), the heat pump runs more efficiently (lower bills), and your home stays comfortable. The recommended sequence is: loft insulation, then cavity wall insulation, then heat pump, then solar panels.

Frequently asked questions

Is loft insulation worth the money?

Yes. £300 to £600 cost, £135 to £355 per year savings, 1 to 4 year payback. Over 40 years, the return is £5,000 to £14,000 at current prices. Calculate your savings.

How much does loft insulation save per year?

£135 (mid-terrace) to £355 (detached) on gas heating. More for oil or LPG. Source: Energy Saving Trust.

Should I do loft insulation myself or get a professional?

DIY costs £150 to £300 in materials and is straightforward for accessible lofts. Professional costs £300 to £600. Free through ECO4 if you qualify.

How thick should loft insulation be?

270mm of mineral wool is the recommended depth. Above 270mm gives diminishing returns.

Does loft insulation make a noticeable difference?

Yes, immediately. A quarter of heat escapes through an uninsulated roof. Rooms below the loft stay warmer, the heating runs less, and bills drop from the first season.
